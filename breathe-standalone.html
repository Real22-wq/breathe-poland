03 — Understanding the Data

What's actually
in the air?

PM2.5
Fine Particulate Matter
Particles smaller than 2.5 micrometres. Can penetrate deep into lungs and enter the bloodstream. Primary driver of cardiovascular and respiratory disease. Main sources: burning coal, wood, vehicle exhaust.
WHO limit: 5 µg/m³ annual mean
Poland avg: 82% above WHO limit
PM10
Coarse Particulate Matter
Larger particles (up to 10 µm). Irritate the upper respiratory tract. From dust, pollen, construction sites, and combustion. Often a visual indicator of smog events.
WHO limit: 15 µg/m³ annual mean
Poland avg: 65% above WHO limit
BaP
Benzo[a]pyrene
A potent carcinogen released from burning coal and wood. Poland has the highest BaP levels in the EU — up to 22× the EU target value in some cities. Directly linked to lung cancer.
EU target: 1 ng/m³
Some Polish cities: 22× above target
NO2
Nitrogen Dioxide
Primarily from vehicle engines and power plants. Causes airway inflammation, worsens asthma and allergies. A major urban issue in Warsaw and other city centres with dense traffic.
WHO limit: 10 µg/m³ annual mean
Warsaw avg: 45% above WHO limit
04 — Milestones

The fight for clean air
in Poland

1970s
Industrialisation peaks. Silesia becomes one of Europe's most polluted regions. Coal mines and steel plants operate without environmental controls.
1989
Political transformation. Democratic Poland inherits a massive industrial pollution legacy. Environmental awareness starts to grow.
2004
EU accession. Poland must begin aligning with European air quality directives. Initial deadlines for compliance set — mostly unmet.
2012
"Smog alarm" concept emerges. Kraków activists launch one of Poland's first civic anti-smog campaigns, bringing the issue to national attention.
2017
Kraków coal ban. Kraków becomes the first Polish city to ban coal and wood burning — a landmark decision later replicated by other cities.
2018
Anti-smog resolutions. Polish parliament passes regional anti-smog regulations, setting emission standards for domestic heating equipment.
2023
EU infringement proceedings. European Commission escalates legal action against Poland for persistent violations of air quality limits.
Now
Progress, but not enough. PM2.5 levels have improved ~20% since 2010, yet Poland still exceeds EU and WHO limits in most regions every winter.
05 — What Now

Clean air is not
a luxury.

Real change requires action at every level — policy, community, and individual. Here's what works, and what you can do right now.

01

Replace your boiler

Old coal boilers ("kopciuchy") are responsible for 80% of residential PM2.5 emissions. Polish government grants cover up to 100% of the cost for low-income households (Program Czyste Powietrze).

Learn about subsidies →
02

Monitor your local air

Install a sensor at home. Platforms like Airly and GIOŚ provide real-time data across Poland. Knowledge is the first step to demanding change from local authorities.

Check Airly →
03

Support policy change

Poland's anti-smog legislation requires strong enforcement. Contact your local councillor. Support organisations like Polski Alarm Smogowy fighting for stronger laws.

Join the movement →
04

Change how you move

Vehicle exhaust is the leading NO₂ source in cities. Cycling, public transport, and EV adoption directly reduce your contribution. Warsaw and Kraków both have growing cycling networks.
